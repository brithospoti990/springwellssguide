Chlorine vs Chloramine in Tap Water

If you installed a softener and your water is soft but still smells like a swimming pool, you're not crazy — and you didn't do anything wrong. You just ran into the thing softeners were never built to fix: the chemical disinfectants in your city water. Here's the difference between the two, and what actually removes them.

I remember a customer calling me, frustrated and confused. He'd just spent thousands on a water softener. His water was soft — but it still smelled like a swimming pool, and it still tasted terrible. "Scott," he asked, "what did I do wrong?" The answer surprised him: he hadn't done anything wrong. He just didn't know that softeners don't remove chlorine.

That conversation happens more than any other, so let me explain what's really in your city water — the two disinfectants utilities use, how they differ, and what you actually need to get rid of them. (For the full city-water playbook, see how to treat hard city water; this article zooms in on the disinfectants themselves.)

The short answer

In one breath

Chlorine and chloramine are disinfectants your utility adds to kill bacteria. Chlorine smells like a pool; chloramine is generally tasteless but harder to remove.

A water softener removes neither. Softeners remove hardness (calcium and magnesium) — not chemicals.

To remove chlorine and chloramine you need a filter — specifically catalytic carbon for chloramine. That's a filter's job, not a softener's.

"A water softener removes hardness. It does NOT remove chlorine or chloramine."

What is chlorine?

Chlorine is the original municipal disinfectant, used in U.S. drinking water since 1908. It's a powerful, fast-acting disinfectant that kills bacteria and viruses effectively — which is exactly why it revolutionized public health and all but eliminated waterborne disease outbreaks in treated systems.

You know chlorine by its distinct "swimming pool" smell and taste. It works fast but gets used up relatively quickly as water travels through the pipes, and when it reacts with organic matter in the water it forms disinfection byproducts — chiefly trihalomethanes (THMs) and haloacetic acids (HAAs), which are regulated because of health concerns at elevated levels. The good news for treatment: standard activated carbon removes free chlorine very effectively, so it's relatively easy to filter out at home.

What is chloramine?

Chloramine is chlorine combined with ammonia, first used in U.S. water in 1929 and increasingly common today. Utilities adopt it because it solves a problem chlorine has: it's more stable and longer-lasting, so it maintains a disinfectant residual all the way to the far ends of the distribution system, and it produces fewer of the traditional THM/HAA byproducts.

How common is it? More than one in five Americans is served by water treated with chloramine, according to the EPA and CDC. Estimates of the share of utilities that have adopted it vary widely by source — commonly cited figures range from around a third to over half — and utilities can switch between chlorine and chloramine for operational reasons, sometimes seasonally, so your water can change without much notice.

Here's the catch, and it's the whole reason this distinction matters: chloramine is generally tasteless and odorless (so you may not smell it), and it's much harder to remove. Standard carbon barely touches it. Removing chloramine requires catalytic carbon — more on that below. It can also form nitrogenous disinfection byproducts, a different class than chlorine's. You can confirm your local details via the CDC and EPA.

A closer look: disinfection byproducts

Disinfectants keep water safe, but they don't act in isolation — they react with the natural organic matter in water, and those reactions create disinfection byproducts (DBPs). It's worth understanding because it's part of why utilities weigh chlorine against chloramine, and part of what a carbon filter helps with.

  • Chlorine tends to form more trihalomethanes (THMs) and haloacetic acids (HAAs) — the two DBP groups the EPA regulates, with limits on total THMs and HAA5 in drinking water because of health concerns at elevated, long-term exposure.
  • Chloramine generally forms fewer of those traditional THMs and HAAs, which is a major reason utilities adopt it — but it can form a different class of nitrogenous byproducts that researchers continue to study.

The practical takeaway isn't alarm — utilities monitor and regulate these, and levels are typically kept within federal limits. It's that a whole-house carbon filter reduces DBPs along with the disinfectant residue itself, which is one more reason filtration is the right tool for the chemical side of your water. For the regulatory detail, the EPA publishes the standards.

What's in your water?

You don't have to guess. Three easy ways to know exactly which disinfectant you have:

  • Read your CCR. Every U.S. community water system publishes a free annual Consumer Confidence Report stating the disinfectant used, plus contaminant detections and often hardness. Usually on your utility's website.
  • Call your utility. Ask directly: "Do you use chlorine or chloramine?" They'll tell you.
  • Test your water. A home test kit can detect chlorine and chloramine along with hardness and other contaminants.

Quick clue: chlorine has that unmistakable pool smell, while chloramine is generally tasteless and odorless. So if your water reeks of pool, it's likely chlorine; if you have water-quality complaints but no strong smell, chloramine is a real suspect — and it's the one that needs catalytic carbon.

Why a softener alone isn't enough

⚠ The insight most guides miss

Why your softener didn't fix your water

If you installed a softener and still notice a chlorine smell in the shower, water that tastes like a pool, dry itchy skin, or bad-tasting drinking water — here's why: water softeners don't remove chlorine or chloramine.

A softener uses ion exchange to remove hardness minerals only — calcium and magnesium. It does nothing to chlorine, chloramine, VOCs, or lead. Your water is genuinely soft; it's just still full of the chemicals a softener was never designed to touch.

Worse, chlorine actively damages your softener. When chlorinated water passes through the resin bed, the chlorine oxidizes the resin beads — breaking down the "glue" that holds them together. Industry guidance commonly cites that chlorine around 1 ppm can cut resin life in half, and some resin manufacturers estimate free chlorine near 1 mg/L can drop resin life to under two years in warmer water. Chlorine is often called the single biggest cause of resin degradation.

The fix: a carbon filter installed before your softener. It removes chlorine and chloramine before the water reaches the resin — giving you chemical-free water throughout the home and protecting your softener's lifespan. Filter first, then softener.

How to remove chlorine & chloramine

Here's the authority-level detail most guides skip: not all carbon filters are the same.

  • Standard activated carbon removes free chlorine very effectively — it's the most common carbon in water filters, and if chlorine is all you have, it does the job.
  • Catalytic carbon is a specially processed carbon that removes both chlorine AND chloramine. Because chloramine is more stable, breaking the chlorine-ammonia bond takes this enhanced carbon — standard carbon simply isn't effective against it.

So the rule is simple: if your utility uses chloramine (and a large share do), you need a whole-house filter built with catalytic carbon. If it uses plain chlorine, standard or catalytic carbon both work — and since utilities can switch, catalytic carbon is the safe, future-proof choice either way.

"Standard carbon removes chlorine. Only catalytic carbon reliably removes chloramine."

Frequently asked questions

What's the difference between chlorine and chloramine?

Both disinfect city water. Chlorine (since 1908) is more potent, smells like a pool, gets used up faster, and forms more THMs/HAAs. Chloramine (chlorine + ammonia, since 1929) is longer-lasting, generally tasteless, forms fewer of those byproducts — but is much harder to remove, needing catalytic carbon.

Why does my water still smell like chlorine after a softener?

Because softeners don't remove chlorine or chloramine — they only remove hardness (calcium and magnesium). Your water is soft but still carries the chemicals. You need a carbon filter for that, ideally installed before the softener.

Do I need a filter if I have a softener?

If your water has chlorine or chloramine (most city water does), yes — a softener leaves those in. A carbon filter removes them and, installed first, also protects the softener's resin from chlorine. A combo does both in one unit.

What removes chloramine?

Catalytic carbon — standard carbon doesn't remove it effectively. If your utility uses chloramine, you need a filter built with catalytic carbon (like the CF1). Boiling and simple pitcher filters generally won't do it.

Does boiling remove chlorine or chloramine?

Boiling can slowly drive off free chlorine but is impractical whole-house, and it does not effectively remove chloramine (far more stable). It also concentrates non-volatile contaminants. A catalytic-carbon filter is the practical solution.

Is it safe to drink water with chlorine or chloramine?

At utility levels, both are considered safe — the EPA/CDC cap is 4 ppm. Many filter anyway for taste and comfort. Note: chloramine must be removed for aquariums and dialysis. General information, not medical advice.

How do I find out which I have?

Read your CCR, call your utility, or test your water. Quick clue: pool smell suggests chlorine; tasteless/odorless problem water suggests chloramine. Utilities can switch, so check your current CCR.

Does chlorine really damage a softener?

Yes — it oxidizes the resin beads. Industry guidance cites ~1 ppm chlorine roughly halving resin life, and some resin makers estimate under two years at ~1 mg/L in warm water. Filtering chlorine out first protects the resin.

My honest take

Here's the whole thing in a nutshell: chlorine and chloramine are the disinfectants that keep your city water safe from bacteria, and they're also why your water smells and tastes the way it does. Chlorine is the potent, pool-smelling original; chloramine is the stable, tasteless, harder-to-remove alternative that a large and growing share of homes now receive. A water softener removes neither — it only handles hardness — which is exactly why so many people install one and are baffled that their water still smells like a pool. To remove these chemicals you need a carbon filter, and if you have chloramine, it must be catalytic carbon. Install it before your softener and you get two wins: chemical-free water everywhere, and a softener protected from the chlorine that would otherwise cut its resin life short. Find out what's in your water with your free CCR, then match the technology to the problem — filter for chemicals, softener for hardness, both when you have both.
