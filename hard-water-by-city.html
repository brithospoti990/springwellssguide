The short answer

What is hard water?

Hard water is water with a high concentration of dissolved calcium and magnesium. As rain and groundwater move through the ground, they pick up these minerals from rock and soil — so how hard your water is depends largely on the geology where it comes from. (Deeper explainer: what is considered hard water?)

Two things worth knowing right away. First, hard water is not a health hazard — calcium and magnesium are dietary minerals, and neither the USGS nor health agencies treat hard water as a drinking risk. Second, it's still a real problem for your home: it leaves scale on fixtures, spots on dishes, shortens appliance life, raises energy bills, and dries out skin and hair. (The full rundown: hard water problems and does hard water damage appliances?) So this is about protecting your house and comfort, not your safety.

Just how widespread is it? By commonly cited estimates, the US average hardness sits somewhere around 130–140 PPM — squarely in the "hard" band — and tens of millions of homes (often cited around 60–65 million) deal with hard water. In other words, if your water is hard, you're in the large majority, not the exception. What varies enormously is how hard, and that's what the map and tables below are for.

Why is the Southwest so hard and the Northwest so soft?

It's not random — it's geology. Water hardness is decided by the rock water flows through before it reaches your tap:

  • The Southwest (Texas, Nevada, Arizona, Southern California): water moves through limestone and chalk rich in calcium and magnesium, which dissolve in and create very hard water.
  • The Pacific Northwest (Oregon, Washington, Northern California): water flows over granite and volcanic rock that release few soluble minerals, so it stays soft — Portland's water is famously near-zero hardness.
  • The Midwest & Great Plains: mixed, but often hard — many cities draw deep mineral-rich groundwater.
  • Florida: the entire state sits on the limestone Floridan Aquifer, producing hard to very hard water nearly statewide.

This is why two homes with identical plumbing and treatment can have completely different water — the rock upstream did the work long before the utility touched it.

How to find your city's exact hardness

Three ways to know for sure

1. Read your CCR. Every US community water system publishes a free annual Consumer Confidence Report, which usually lists hardness. Find it on your utility's website.

2. Call your utility. Ask directly: "What's our average hardness in PPM or GPG?" They'll tell you.

3. Test your water. The most accurate method for your tap — a home test kit gives your exact hardness (and other contaminants), which is what you'd size a softener around. City averages vary by neighborhood and even by street.

Frequently asked questions

How do I know if my city's water is hard?

Read your CCR, call your utility, or test your water — the test is most accurate for your tap. As a benchmark, ~85% of US homes have at least moderately hard water. Published city figures are approximate averages; your own test gives the exact number.

What is the hardest water in the US?

Generally the Southwest, West, and Great Plains. Cities often cited among the hardest include Midland TX (~400–600 mg/L), Las Vegas, and Ventura CA (reported as high as ~666). These are reported figures that vary — treat rankings loosely; the regional pattern is the reliable part.

What do PPM and GPG mean?

PPM (mg/L) and GPG measure hardness; 1 GPG ≈ 17.1 PPM. USGS bands: soft 0–60, moderately hard 61–120, hard 121–180, very hard 180+ PPM. Higher numbers mean more scale, more appliance wear, and drier skin.

Is hard water safe to drink?

Yes — it's not a health hazard. Calcium and magnesium are dietary minerals. Hard water's problems are practical (scale, appliance wear, dry skin), not medical. Contaminant concerns are a separate, filter/RO question.

Can hard water damage my appliances?

Yes — scale coats heating elements and narrows pipes, making water heaters, dishwashers, and washers work harder and wear out faster. The harder the water, the faster it happens, which is why very hard water makes a softener a money-saver.

Do I need a water softener?

Soft (under 60): no. Moderate (61–120): optional. Hard (121–180): recommended. Very hard (180+): strongly recommended. Confirm your hardness first — look up your city, then test your tap.

Why is water harder in some regions?

Geology. Limestone-rich areas (Southwest, Great Plains, Florida) produce hard water; granite/volcanic areas (Pacific Northwest, parts of the Northeast) produce soft water. The rock upstream sets your hardness before treatment.

Does city data match my tap exactly?

Roughly, not exactly — utilities blend sources, and hardness varies by area and season. Treat published figures as approximate averages and test your own tap for the precise number, especially for sizing a softener.

My honest take

Here's the whole thing in a nutshell: about 85% of US homes have hard water, and where you live largely decides how hard — the limestone Southwest, Great Plains, and Florida run hard to very hard, while the granite Pacific Northwest runs soft. Hard water won't hurt your health, but it quietly costs you through scale, shortened appliance life, higher energy bills, and dry skin. Use the tools and tables here to place your city on the USGS scale and get oriented — but remember every published city number is an approximate average that shifts by neighborhood and season. The only figure that truly matters for choosing and sizing equipment is your own tap test. Get that number, match it to the scale, and you'll know exactly whether you can enjoy your water as-is or whether a softener will pay for itself.
