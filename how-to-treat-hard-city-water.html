How to Treat Hard City Water: The Complete Guide

If your city water smells like a pool, leaves scale on the faucets, and dries out your skin, you're fighting two different problems at once — and they need two different solutions. Here's exactly what's in municipal water, what removes what, and the one installation detail that protects your equipment.

I remember the day I finally realized my city water had two problems. I'd installed a softener — my water was soft, but it still smelled like a pool, and it still tasted terrible. My skin felt better, but I clearly wasn't done. That's when I learned the truth: city water is disinfected, not purified. It has chemicals and hardness, and you need two different solutions to fix both.

Let me walk you through what's really in your municipal water, which technology removes each thing, and the one installation detail that quietly protects your softener from early failure. If you want the pure concept first — any filter vs any softener — see my filter vs softener guide; this one is specifically about treating city water.

The short answer

In one breath

City water has two problems: chemical disinfectants (chlorine or chloramine) and hardness minerals (calcium and magnesium).

It needs two solutions: a filter for the chemicals, a softener for the hardness. They are not interchangeable.

Most city homes need both — and a combo system does both in one unit, in the correct order.

"City water is disinfected, not purified. It kills bacteria but leaves behind chemical residues."

Problem #1: the disinfectants (chlorine & chloramine)

Every municipal supply is disinfected, and that disinfectant residue is what you smell and taste. There are two kinds, and the difference matters for how you treat it:

  • Free chlorine — the classic "pool" disinfectant. Effective, but it off-gasses and reacts with organics to form disinfection byproducts. Standard carbon removes it well.
  • Chloramine — chlorine combined with ammonia, used because it's more stable and lasts longer in the pipes. A large and growing share of U.S. utilities have switched to it (commonly cited estimates put it around two-thirds of systems, serving well over 100 million people). The catch: it's much harder to remove, and standard carbon barely touches it.

This is the single most important city-water distinction, and most guides skip it: removing chloramine requires catalytic carbon, a specially processed carbon that breaks the chloramine bond. If your utility uses chloramine (your water report will say), a filter built with catalytic carbon isn't optional — it's the only thing that works. For the science and independent lab data, see my SpringWell CF1 review. You can confirm your disinfectant and other details through the EPA and CDC.

Problem #2: hardness minerals

Municipal treatment doesn't remove calcium and magnesium — the minerals that make water "hard" — and levels vary widely by region. Water is generally considered hard above 7 grains per gallon (GPG). (Full scale: what is considered hard water?) Hardness is a completely separate problem from disinfectants, and it shows up differently:

  • Scale — that white, crusty buildup on faucets, showerheads, and inside your pipes and water heater
  • Appliance wear — scale shortens the life of water heaters, dishwashers, and washing machines
  • Dry skin & dull hair, stiff laundry, and soap that won't lather
  • Spots on dishes and glassware

No filter fixes any of this — hardness passes right through carbon. The only thing that removes it is a water softener, which uses ion exchange to swap the calcium and magnesium for a little sodium. (Mechanics: how do water softeners work?; the damage it prevents: hard water problems.)

Why order matters: filter FIRST

⚠ The detail most guides miss

If you install both a filter and a softener, the filter must go first — and this isn't just a preference, it protects your equipment. Chlorine degrades a softener's ion-exchange resin. Industry guidance commonly cites that chlorine around 1 ppm can roughly halve the resin's life.

Put the carbon filter before the softener and it strips the chlorine out before it ever touches the resin — so your softener lasts its full lifespan. Put them in the wrong order, and chlorinated water attacks the resin day after day, aging it prematurely. In a combo system this correct order is built in; with separate units, place the filter upstream and the softener downstream.

Can you drink softened city water?

The drinking-water distinction

Because a softener swaps calcium and magnesium for a little sodium, softened water carries a small amount of added sodium (roughly 12–28 mg per glass depending on hardness — less than a slice of bread).

For most healthy people, it's fine to drink. But a common approach is to keep a reverse-osmosis tap or an unsoftened line for drinking and cooking, especially on a sodium-restricted diet. For the purest drinking water — sodium plus lead and other contaminants removed — pair your system with an under-sink RO unit. Full detail: is softened water safe to drink? How to get started

Three simple steps before you buy anything:

  • 1. Read your CCR. Every U.S. community water system publishes a free annual Consumer Confidence Report listing your disinfectant (chlorine or chloramine), detected contaminants, and often hardness. It's usually on your utility's website — the fastest way to learn what you're treating.
  • 2. Test your water. For hardness specifically, an inexpensive home test kit gives you a precise GPG number so you can size a softener correctly, and confirms contaminants the CCR may not detail for your exact tap.
  • 3. Choose your system. Chemicals only → the CF1 filter. Hardness only → the SS1 softener. Both (most city homes) → the Combo.

Frequently asked questions

How do I treat hard city water?

Solve both problems: a carbon filter (catalytic carbon if you have chloramine) for the chemicals, and a softener for the hardness. They're not interchangeable. Most city homes have both issues and need both — a combo does it in one unit. Read your CCR and test your water first.

Do I need a filter if I have a softener?

Often yes — a softener removes hardness but not chlorine or chloramine, so softened water can still smell and taste of pool. A carbon filter fixes that, and filtering chlorine out first also protects your softener's resin. Run the filter first, then the softener.

What removes chloramine?

Catalytic carbon — standard carbon barely touches it. Since a large share of utilities use chloramine, a filter built with catalytic carbon (like the CF1) is essential if your city uses it. Your CCR states which disinfectant you have.

Why does my water smell like a pool?

That's the chlorine or chloramine your utility adds to disinfect it — doing its job, but leaving a residue you smell, taste, and feel (it dries skin). A whole-house carbon filter removes it. Scale and spots are a separate hardness problem needing a softener.

Which installs first?

The filter, then the softener — chlorine degrades softener resin (about 1 ppm can roughly halve its life), so filter it out first. Combos build this order in; with separate units, put the filter upstream.

Can I drink softened water?

For most healthy people, yes — it adds only a little sodium (~12–28 mg/glass, less than bread). Many keep an RO tap or unsoftened line for drinking, especially on sodium-restricted diets. Not medical advice.

How much does it cost?

Roughly: CF1 ~$830, SS1 ~$1,549, Combo ~$2,250 (larger packages higher), before installation. A softener also pays back over time via appliance life and energy/soap savings. Verify current pricing.

What's a CCR and how do I get it?

A Consumer Confidence Report — the free annual water-quality report your utility must provide, listing your disinfectant, contaminants, and often hardness. Find it on your utility's website. For a precise hardness number, add an inexpensive home test.

My honest take

Here's the whole thing in a nutshell: city water is disinfected, not purified, so it arrives with two separate problems — chemical disinfectants and hardness minerals. A filter removes the chemicals (catalytic carbon if you're on chloramine, which a large share of homes are); a softener removes the hardness. They are not interchangeable, and most city homes have both problems, which is why a combo — installed filter-first to protect the resin — is so often the right answer. Whatever you choose, start free: read your CCR to see what your utility puts in your water, then test for a precise hardness number. Match the technology to the actual problem, get the order right, and you'll finally have clean, great-tasting, soft water from every tap — no more pool smell, no more scale.
