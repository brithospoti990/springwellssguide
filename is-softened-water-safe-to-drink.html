Is Softened Water Safe to Drink? The Honest Sodium Facts

"Is softened water safe to drink? I've heard it adds sodium, and I'm worried about my blood pressure." It's the #1 question that keeps health-conscious homeowners from buying a softener — and the one salespeople dodge. Here's the honest answer, with real data and a clear solution.

The question I hear more than almost any other is: "Scott, is softened water safe to drink? I've heard it adds sodium — and I'm worried about my blood pressure." It's a fair question. It's the single biggest objection that keeps health-conscious homeowners from buying a water softener, and it's the question most salespeople either dodge or wave away.

I'm going to give you the honest answer — with real data, not marketing reassurance. I'll show you exactly how much sodium we're talking about, who genuinely needs to be cautious, and the elegant solution for anyone who wants soft water and sodium-free drinking water. One note up front: this is general information, not medical advice — for your specific health situation, your doctor is the right call.

The short answer

The straight answer

Yes, a salt-based softener adds sodium to your water — but for most people, the amount is small and safe. The Mayo Clinic puts a glass of softened water at under 12.5 mg of sodium — less than a slice of bread.

For most healthy people, it poses no health concern. For those on sodium-restricted diets or with certain conditions, it's worth managing — and there's a simple solution.

That solution is reverse osmosis (RO): an under-sink RO system removes up to 98% of the sodium, so you can have soft water everywhere and pure drinking water. Let me show you the data.

How sodium gets into softened water

Quick context. A salt-based softener works by ion exchange: as hard water passes through resin beads, the beads grab the calcium and magnesium (the hardness) and release a small amount of sodium in their place. (Full mechanics: how do water softeners work?)

So the sodium isn't an additive or a contaminant — it's a direct, one-for-one consequence of removing the hardness. And here's the key fact that the rest of this article rests on: the amount of sodium added is proportional to how hard your water was. Harder water means more minerals removed, which means a bit more sodium. That's why the honest answer depends on your specific hardness — so let's get specific.

How much sodium is actually added?

This is the number you came for, and most guides dodge it. Here are the facts, straight from trusted sources:

The sodium data

Sodium added scales with hardness — about 1.86 mg per 8-oz glass for each GPG of hardness removed:

  • Mayo Clinic: a glass generally contains under 12.5 mg of sodium — within the FDA's "very low sodium" definition (≤35 mg/serving)
  • At 10 GPG hardness: about 18.8 mg per glass
  • At 18 GPG hardness: about 35 mg per glass

To put that in perspective:

  • A slice of white bread: ~150 mg
  • A tablespoon of ketchup: ~204 mg
  • A 250 ml glass of softened water: about 1% of your daily sodium intake

Is it safe for most people? Yes.

The data above is reassuring, and so is the expert consensus. The Australian Drinking Water Guidelines note that softened water is unlikely to present a health concern. A widely cited 1997 study in the Archives of Internal Medicine concluded that it's unlikely the increased sodium from softened water would pose health risks for most people.

In plain terms: if you're a generally healthy adult, drinking softened water at typical hardness is fine. The sodium it adds is a rounding error next to what you already get from food — and far below the levels that would concern a doctor for someone without a specific condition. For most families, the sodium question simply shouldn't stand between you and solving your hard water.

Who should be cautious?

"Most people" isn't everyone, and I won't pretend otherwise. The honest exceptions — the people who genuinely should pay attention — are:

⚠ Consult your doctor if you have…
  • A severely sodium-restricted diet
  • Hypertension (high blood pressure) that's sodium-sensitive
  • Kidney disease
  • Severe heart conditions (such as heart failure)

The EPA's sodium guidance of 20 mg/L in drinking water is aimed specifically at "the most at-risk segment of the population." If you fall into one of these categories, talk to your doctor before regularly drinking softened water — and strongly consider the reverse-osmosis solution below, which removes the sodium entirely. (General information, not medical advice.)

There's also one specific case worth calling out: infant formula. Many authorities advise against making up baby formula with softened water, because infants are sensitive to sodium and formula is already balanced for them. Use an unsoftened bypass tap, low-sodium bottled water, or RO water for formula — and follow your pediatrician's guidance.

The scary number, in context

If you've researched this, you may have seen an alarming figure: that 1997 study found sodium levels as high as 1,219 mg/L in some households, with about 17% above 400 mg/L. That sounds frightening — so let me give you the context that makes it useful instead of scary.

Those high numbers come from very hard water requiring heavy sodium addition — the harder the water, the more sodium softening adds. A reading like 1,219 mg/L implies extraordinarily hard source water that most homes simply don't have. For typical hardness, levels are far lower — often well under 50 mg per glass, as the calculator above shows. The honest takeaway isn't "panic"; it's "know your number." If you have very hard water and a sodium concern, test your water, and add an RO tap — which removes up to 98% of the sodium regardless of where you start.

The elegant solution: reverse osmosis

Soft water everywhere, pure water to drink

Here's the answer that lets you stop choosing between soft water and your health: a reverse-osmosis (RO) system installed at your kitchen sink removes up to 98% of the sodium from softened water — along with lead, chlorine, microplastics, and other contaminants.

And the beautiful part: a softener and an RO system work better together. The softener removes the hard minerals before water reaches the RO membrane, which protects that membrane from scaling and meaningfully extends its life. (That's why RO is installed after the softener in the water's path.)

The result? Soft water for your whole house — bathrooms, laundry, appliances — and pure, near-sodium-free water from your kitchen tap for drinking and cooking. It's the complete solution for the health-conscious homeowner.

Don't want to add RO? Potassium chloride is another route — it swaps the sodium for potassium in the softening process, adding zero sodium, though it costs more and isn't right for everyone (I cover the full trade-offs in potassium chloride vs sodium chloride). For most people who want sodium-free drinking water, though, RO is the cleaner, more flexible answer.

Frequently asked questions

Is softened water safe to drink?

For most healthy people, yes — a glass has under ~12.5 mg sodium (Mayo Clinic), less than a slice of bread, within the FDA's "very low sodium" range. Those on sodium-restricted diets or with hypertension/kidney/heart conditions should consult a doctor and consider an RO tap. Not medical advice.

How much sodium does it add?

About 1.86 mg per 8-oz glass per GPG of hardness — ~18.8 mg at 10 GPG, ~35 mg at 18 GPG. For context, bread is ~150 mg and a glass is ~1% of your daily intake. Negligible for most.

Can I remove the sodium?

Yes — a reverse-osmosis system at the kitchen sink removes up to 98% of sodium (plus lead, chlorine, microplastics). Installed after the softener, it gives soft water everywhere and pure drinking water, and the softener protects the RO membrane.

Is it safe with high blood pressure?

For many with managed blood pressure the added sodium is minor, but on a restricted diet it's worth managing — discuss with your doctor. The simplest fix is an RO drinking tap (removes up to 98%). Not medical advice.

Is it safe for babies / formula?

Caution advised: it's generally recommended not to make infant formula with softened water due to sodium. Use an unsoftened bypass tap, low-sodium bottled water, or RO water — and follow your pediatrician. RO or a bypass line solves this easily.

Can I cook with softened water?

For most people, yes — and it avoids scale in pots and kettles. On a strict sodium restriction, you may prefer RO or unsoftened water for absorbed water (rice, pasta). An RO tap covers both drinking and cooking.

How does RO remove sodium?

It pushes water through a membrane whose pores are too fine for dissolved sodium ions to pass, removing up to ~98%. Under-sink after the softener, it delivers pure drinking water while the softener keeps the membrane from scaling.

What about studies showing very high sodium?

A 1997 study found up to 1,219 mg/L in some homes (17% above 400 mg/L) — but those reflect very hard water needing heavy sodium addition, which most homes don't have. Typical levels are far lower. Test your water; RO removes up to 98% regardless.

My honest take

So, is softened water safe to drink? For the great majority of people, yes — a glass adds less sodium than a slice of bread, well within "very low sodium" territory, and health authorities consider it unlikely to be a concern. Sodium chloride softening is safe for most households, full stop. If you're on a sodium-restricted diet, have hypertension, kidney, or heart conditions, or you're making infant formula, then it's worth managing — and the elegant answer is reverse osmosis at the kitchen sink, which removes up to 98% of the sodium and gives you pure drinking water while the rest of your home enjoys soft water. You don't have to choose between soft water and your health; with a softener plus RO, you get both. Test your water, weigh your situation, and stop letting the sodium question keep your hard water in charge.
