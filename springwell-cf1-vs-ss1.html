SpringWell CF1 vs SS1: Filter or Softener First?

You've settled on SpringWell — but now you're stuck between the CF1 filter and the SS1 softener. They sound similar; they're not. Let me show you exactly what each one does, help you diagnose which you need, and — if money's tight — tell you honestly which to buy first.

I get asked this all the time: "Scott, should I buy the CF1 or the SS1 — or do I need both?" It's a great question, and one far too many homeowners get wrong. They buy a filter when they need a softener. They buy a softener when they need a filter. Or they buy one and wonder why their water still isn't right.

So let me clear it up — with the actual specs, a symptom diagnostic, and straight talk on the budget question nobody else answers: if you can only afford one to start, which one? If you want the concept-level version first (any filter vs any softener), see my filter vs softener guide. This one is specifically about SpringWell's two systems.

The short answer

In one breath

The CF1 is a filter. It removes contaminants — chlorine, chloramine, VOCs, lead, PFAS, sediment — for cleaner, safer, better-tasting water.

The SS1 is a softener. It removes hardness — calcium and magnesium — for no scale, better lather, softer skin, and longer-lasting appliances.

They are not the same thing, and they don't do each other's jobs. Most homes — with hard and contaminated water — need both, which is exactly what the SpringWell Combo delivers in one system.

"CF1 removes contaminants. SS1 removes hardness. They are not the same thing."

The SpringWell CF1, explained

The CF1 is SpringWell's whole-house filter — built for city water. As water passes through its 4-stage ActivFlo design, each stage strips out something you don't want:

  • KDF media — reduces chlorine and heavy metals
  • Catalytic carbon — the key stage: it breaks down chloramine, which standard carbon barely touches (and 100M+ Americans have chloramine-treated water), plus VOCs, pesticides, and herbicides
  • Flex bed — eliminates channeling and increases contact time
  • 5-micron sediment filter — removes sand, silt, and clay

In independent testing the results are strong: THMs dropped from 31.8 ppb to non-detectable, with 96% lead removal and 95%+ chlorine removal. It carries a 1,000,000-gallon capacity (roughly a decade for an average family), 9–17 GPM flow, and a lifetime warranty — around $830 factory-direct. What it does not do is soften: it won't remove the calcium and magnesium that cause scale. Full write-up: my SpringWell CF1 review.

The SpringWell SS1, explained

The SS1 is SpringWell's whole-house salt-based softener. Using ion exchange, it swaps the calcium and magnesium in your water for a little sodium as water flows through a 10% cross-linked resin bed (the more durable, gold-standard resin). The payoff is a documented 97.5% hardness reduction — meaning:

  • No more scale on faucets, showerheads, and inside pipes
  • Spot-free dishes, better soap lather, softer skin, hair, and laundry
  • Longer appliance life — water heaters, dishwashers, and washers

It comes in 32,000–80,000 grain sizes to match your household, flows 9–20 GPM, uses a Bluetooth digital control head with metered demand regeneration, and carries a lifetime warranty — around $1,549 factory-direct.

Iron, done honestly: the SS1 softener handles up to ~3 PPM of clear-water iron on its own as a bonus of ion exchange — fine for light iron. It does not remove 7 PPM. If your well runs higher (up to 7 PPM), you need SpringWell's well configuration, which adds dedicated iron filtration (plus sulfur and manganese). Don't rely on a bare softener — or the CF1 — for real well-water iron. See best system for well water with iron.

What the SS1 does not do is filter: it won't remove chlorine, chloramine, lead, or other contaminants. That's the CF1's job — which is the whole reason this comparison exists. (The SS1 is the system I review in depth on my homepage softener review.)

Which one first? (the budget question)

If you can only buy one to start

Plenty of homeowners need both but can't swing both at once. That's fine — here's my honest rule: buy the one that solves your most pressing daily problem first, then add the other later. There's no universally "correct" first system; it depends on what's bothering you most.

Start with the CF1 if…your biggest complaints are taste, smell, or contaminant/health worries (chlorine, lead, PFAS). Clean, safe water first; add softening later.
Start with the SS1 if…your biggest complaints are scale, spots, dry skin, or appliance wear. Stop the hardness damage first; add filtration later.

One caveat worth the math: if you know you'll buy both within a year or two, the Combo is usually better value than purchasing the CF1 and SS1 separately over time — and it guarantees the correct filter-first order. Phasing makes sense when cash flow requires it, not as the cheaper end state.

If you buy both separately — which goes first?

Installation order: the CF1 goes first, then the SS1. Sediment and chlorine can shorten the life of the softener's ion-exchange resin, so filtering them out before the water reaches the SS1 protects the resin. In the Combo this order is built in; with separate units, place the CF1 upstream (closer to where water enters) and the SS1 downstream.

Can you drink softened water from the SS1?

The drinking-water distinction

Because the SS1 swaps calcium and magnesium for a little sodium, softened water carries a small amount of added sodium (roughly 12–28 mg per glass depending on hardness — less than a slice of bread).

For most healthy people, it's fine to drink. But a common approach is to keep a reverse-osmosis tap or an unsoftened line for drinking and cooking — especially on a sodium-restricted diet. Softened water is excellent for bathing, laundry, and appliances; for the purest drinking water, pair your system with an under-sink RO unit.

Frequently asked questions

What's the difference between CF1 and SS1?

CF1 is a filter — it removes contaminants (chlorine, chloramine, VOCs, lead, PFAS, sediment). SS1 is a softener — it removes hardness (calcium and magnesium) via ion exchange. CF1 doesn't soften; SS1 doesn't filter. Different problems, different technologies.

Do I need CF1, SS1, or both?

Scale, spots, dry skin → SS1. Chlorine smell, bad taste, contaminant worry → CF1. Symptoms from both lists (common) → both, via the Combo. Test your water first to confirm.

Which should I buy first?

Buy the one that solves your most pressing problem first: CF1 if it's taste/smell/health; SS1 if it's scale/skin/appliances. Add the other later. If you'll buy both within a year or two, the Combo is usually better value than phasing.

Can I use CF1 without SS1 (or vice versa)?

Yes — each works fully on its own and addresses an independent problem. Run just one if you only have one issue: CF1 for soft-but-chlorinated city water, SS1 for clean-but-hard water.

Does the SS1 remove iron?

Up to ~3 PPM clear-water iron as a bonus of ion exchange — fine for light iron, but not 7 PPM. For higher well-water iron (up to 7 PPM) you need SpringWell's well configuration with dedicated iron filtration; the CF1 doesn't remove well-water iron either. Test your well first.

Can I drink softened water?

For most healthy people, yes — the SS1 adds only a little sodium (~12–28 mg/glass, less than bread). Many keep an RO tap or unsoftened line for drinking, especially on sodium-restricted diets. Not medical advice.

Which installs first?

The CF1, then the SS1 — sediment and chlorine can shorten softener resin life, so filter them out first. The Combo builds this order in; with separate units, put the CF1 upstream.

Is the Combo just a CF1 and SS1 together?

Essentially yes — integrated filtration + softening in one system, in the correct order, with space savings, one maintenance point, and usually better value than buying the two standalone over time.

My honest take

Here's the whole thing in a nutshell: the CF1 and SS1 are not the same, and they don't do each other's jobs. The CF1 filters — it makes your water clean, safe, and great-tasting. The SS1 softens — it ends scale and protects your skin, hair, and appliances. If your symptoms sit in one group, buy that one system and save your money. If they span both — scale and chlorine, dry skin and bad taste — you need both, and the Combo is the space-saving, better-value way to get there. If cash flow means phasing, start with whatever bothers you most and add the other later. And on iron, don't let anyone tell you a bare softener removes 7 PPM — the SS1 handles light iron (~3 PPM); real well-water iron needs the well system. Test your water, match the system to the actual problem, and you'll never be the person who bought the wrong one and wondered why the water still isn't right.
