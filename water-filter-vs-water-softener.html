Buying Guide · Filter vs Softener

Do You Need a Water Filter AND a Softener?

It's the confusion I hear most: people use "filter" and "softener" as if they're the same thing, buy the wrong one, and wonder why their water still isn't right. Let me clear it up once and for all — what each system does, how to diagnose your water, and whether you need one or both.

The question I get asked more than almost any other is: "Scott, what's the difference between a water filter and a water softener — and which one do I need?" It's a great question, and far too many homeowners get it wrong. They buy a filter when they need a softener. They buy a softener when they need a filter. Or they buy one, and wonder why their water still isn't right.

So let me clear this up once and for all — in plain English, with a simple way to diagnose your own water. By the end, you'll know exactly whether you need a filter, a softener, or both, and you won't waste a dollar on the wrong system.

The short answer

In one breath

A water filter removes contaminants — chlorine, chloramine, VOCs, lead, PFAS, sediment. It makes water cleaner, safer, and better-tasting.

A water softener removes hardness minerals — calcium and magnesium. It makes water soft: no scale, better lather, longer-lasting appliances, kinder skin and hair.

They are not the same thing, and they don't do each other's jobs. A filter won't soften; a softener won't clean. Many homes — with both hard and contaminated water — need both.

"A filter removes contaminants. A softener removes hardness. They are not the same thing."

What a water filter does

A water filter is about purity and safety. As water passes through filtration media — typically activated or catalytic carbon, sometimes reverse osmosis — the media captures or breaks down the things you don't want in your water:

  • Chlorine and chloramine — the disinfectants that make water smell and taste "like a pool"
  • VOCs, pesticides, and herbicides — dissolved organic chemicals
  • Lead and other heavy metals — a health concern in many older systems
  • PFAS — the persistent "forever chemicals"
  • Sediment and rust — the particles that cloud water and clog fixtures
  • Bacteria and viruses — with the right type (e.g. a UV stage) for well water

The payoff: water that's cleaner, safer, and genuinely better-tasting from every tap. What a filter does not do is remove the dissolved calcium and magnesium that make water hard. (For a deep example, see my SpringWell CF1 review.)

What a water softener does

A water softener is about hardness — nothing else. Using a process called ion exchange, a salt-based softener swaps the calcium and magnesium in your water for a small amount of sodium as the water passes through a resin bed. (Full mechanics: how do water softeners work?) Removing that hardness delivers:

  • No more scale on faucets, showerheads, and inside pipes
  • Spot-free dishes and glassware
  • Better soap lather — you'll use less soap and detergent
  • Softer skin and hair, and softer laundry
  • Longer appliance life — water heaters, dishwashers, and washing machines last longer without scale

The payoff: soft, scale-free water throughout your home. What a softener does not do is remove chlorine, lead, or any other contaminant — it only handles hardness. This is the crux of the confusion, so let me show it visually.

It comes down to three honest paths:

The three paths

Match your problems to the right path — no more, no less.

Filter onlyYour water is contaminated (chlorine, taste, lead) but not hard. You need clean water, not soft water.
Softener onlyYour water is hard (scale, spots, dry skin) but clean and good-tasting. You need soft water, not filtration.
BothYou have symptoms from both lists — contamination and hardness. This is the most common case, and a combo is the elegant fix.

Whichever path you're on, start by testing your water — it confirms your hardness and contaminant levels so you buy exactly what you need.

Is softened water safe to drink?

The drinking-water distinction

A common question — and an important distinction. Because softeners swap calcium and magnesium for a little sodium, softened water carries a small amount of added sodium (roughly 12–28 mg per glass depending on hardness — less than a slice of bread).

For most healthy people, softened water is fine to drink. But a common, sensible approach is to keep a reverse-osmosis (RO) tap or an unsoftened line for drinking and cooking water — especially if anyone in the home is on a sodium-restricted diet. Softened water is excellent for bathing, laundry, and appliances; for the purest drinking water, RO removes the sodium along with contaminants.

The combo solution (when you need both)

Two problems, one system

If your home has both hard-water problems (scale, dry skin, appliance wear) and water-quality concerns (chlorine, bad taste, contaminants), you need both a filter and a softener — and the elegant way to get them is a combo system.

One integrated unit filters and softens your water in sequence: it saves utility-room space (fewer tanks), simplifies maintenance to one system, and is often priced similarly to buying the two separately. It delivers the complete transformation — clean AND soft water from every tap.

Which goes first — filter or softener?

Installation order: the filter goes first, then the softener. Sediment and chlorine can damage or shorten the life of a softener's ion-exchange resin, so filtering them out before the water reaches the softener protects the resin and keeps everything running at its best. In a combo system this correct order is built in; if you install separate systems, place the whole-house filter upstream and the softener downstream of it.

3 costly mistakes I see homeowners make

After years of these conversations, the same expensive mix-ups come up again and again. Avoid these three and you're most of the way to the right decision:

  • Buying a softener to fix bad-tasting or smelly water. This is the most common one. Someone's water tastes like chlorine, they install a softener, and they're baffled when the taste is unchanged — because a softener doesn't touch chlorine. That was a filter problem all along. If your complaint is taste or smell, you need filtration, not softening.
  • Buying a filter to fix scale and spots. The mirror image. Someone's fixtures are crusted with scale and their glasses are spotty, so they install a whole-house carbon filter — and the scale keeps coming, because the filter doesn't remove the calcium and magnesium causing it. That's a softener's job. Filtered water can still be perfectly hard.
  • Buying only one when they clearly needed both. Plenty of homes — especially on hard municipal water that's also chlorinated — have symptoms from both lists. Buying a single system solves half the problem and leaves the person half-disappointed. If you've got scale and chlorine, or dry skin and bad taste, one system was never going to be enough.

The through-line in all three: match the technology to the actual problem. That's the entire point of this guide — and the reason I always push testing your water before buying anything. Guessing is how these mistakes happen.

Frequently asked questions

What's the difference between a filter and a softener?

A filter removes contaminants (chlorine, chloramine, VOCs, lead, PFAS, sediment); a softener removes hardness minerals (calcium and magnesium) via ion exchange. A filter doesn't soften, and a softener doesn't clean — different problems, different technologies. Many homes need both.

Do I need a filter, a softener, or both?

Scale, spots, poor lather, or dry skin → softener. Chlorine smell, bad taste, or contaminant worries → filter. Symptoms from both lists (very common) → both. Test your water first to confirm hardness and contaminant levels.

Can I drink softened water?

For most healthy people, yes — it adds only a little sodium (~12–28 mg/glass, less than bread). Many keep an RO tap or unsoftened line for drinking, especially on sodium-restricted diets. Not medical advice.

Which do I install first?

The filter, then the softener — sediment and chlorine can damage softener resin, so filter them out first. Combo systems build this order in; with separate units, put the filter upstream.

Can one system do both?

Yes — a combo system filters and softens in one integrated unit, saving space and simplifying maintenance, often at similar cost to buying both separately. Ideal if you have both problems; overkill if you only have one.

How much do they cost?

Roughly: filter ~$800–$3,000 installed; softener ~$1,200–$3,000 installed; combo ~$2,100–$3,300. A softener tends to pay back over time via appliance life, energy, and soap savings. Verify current pricing.

Will a softener remove chlorine or lead?

No — a standard softener only removes hardness, not chlorine, lead, or other contaminants. For those you need a filter. Matching the technology to the problem is the whole point.

Does a filter make water soft?

No — a standard filter removes contaminants but not the calcium and magnesium that cause hardness, so filtered water can still be hard. Only a softener (or salt-free conditioner for scale) addresses hardness.

My honest take

Here's the whole thing in a nutshell: a filter and a softener are not the same, and they don't do each other's jobs. A filter removes contaminants — it makes your water clean, safe, and great-tasting. A softener removes hardness — it makes your water soft, ending scale and protecting your skin, hair, and appliances. If your symptoms fall in one group, buy one system and save your money. If they span both — scale and chlorine, dry skin and bad taste — you need both, and a combo is the space-saving, simplified way to get there. For drinking, keep in mind the small sodium a softener adds, and consider an RO tap if that matters to you. Above all, test your water first so you match the solution to the real problem. Do that, and you'll never be the person who bought the wrong system and wondered why the water still isn't right.
