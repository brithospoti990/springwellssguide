What Is Considered Hard Water? GPG & ppm Chart

White spots, poor soap lather, scale on the faucets — and a water report full of confusing numbers. Some say ppm, some say mg/L, softener companies say GPG. Here's the definitive hardness scale, translated into plain English.

Scott Murley Founder · water treatment writer & former install tech
Updated
June 21, 2026

What is considered hard water?

Hard water is water with a high level of dissolved calcium and magnesium. Water is considered hard at 7 grains per gallon (GPG) or 120 ppm and above, and very hard at 10.5 GPG or 180 ppm and above. Below 1 GPG (17 ppm) is soft. Hardness affects scale and cleaning, not safety.

If you've got white spots on your dishes, soap that won't lather, scale creeping onto the faucets, and maybe dry-feeling skin after a shower — and then you pull up your water report and it's a wall of numbers in units you don't recognize — you're in exactly the right place. The confusion is real: city reports and lab results usually list hardness in ppm or mg/L, while softener companies talk in grains per gallon (GPG). Same thing, different units, and nobody tells you how to translate. Let me fix that, and tell you what the number actually means for your home.

When I look at a water test, I don't start by asking what system to sell. I start by asking what the number actually means for the home — because that's the only thing that tells you whether you have a minor annoyance or a real, money-costing scale problem.

What is considered hard water?

In plain English: hard water is water that contains a lot of dissolved calcium and magnesium. These minerals get picked up as water moves through limestone and mineral-rich ground, which is why hard water is so common across much of the U.S. (according to the USGS, most American homes have at least moderately hard water).

Here's the reassuring part most people want to hear first: hardness is not usually dangerous. The EPA doesn't regulate hardness as a health contaminant, because calcium and magnesium aren't harmful to drink — they're actually dietary minerals. What hardness does cause is scale and maintenance problems: deposits on fixtures and inside appliances, poor soap performance, spotty dishes, and gradual buildup in your plumbing and water heater. So think of it as a comfort-and-cost issue, not a safety scare.

How to convert ppm to GPG (and back)

The whole translation comes down to one number: 1 grain per gallon = 17.1 ppm (mg/L) as calcium carbonate. So:

The two formulas

GPG = ppm ÷ 17.1  |  ppm = GPG × 17.1

A few worked examples so it's concrete:

  • 120 ppm ÷ 17.1 ≈ 7 GPG — the start of "hard"
  • 180 ppm ÷ 17.1 ≈ 10.5 GPG — the start of "very hard"
  • 250 ppm ÷ 17.1 ≈ 14.6 GPG — solidly very hard

A couple of practical notes: ppm and mg/L are treated as the same for water hardness reporting, so don't let the two labels confuse you. GPG is what softener companies and sizing tools use (it's the unit softeners are rated in), while ppm/mg/L is what you'll see on lab reports and city water reports. What hard water feels like in a real home

Numbers are abstract until you connect them to what you're actually seeing. Here's how hardness shows up, room by room:

Kitchen

White spots on dishes and cloudy glassware out of the dishwasher, chalky residue around the sink, scale on the faucet and inside the kettle and coffee maker. (See hard water spots on dishes.)

Bathroom

Soap scum on the shower glass and tile, crusty buildup on showerheads and faucets, and that film that won't rinse away. (See soap scum and hard water stains.)

Laundry

Stiff, scratchy towels, dingy whites, and needing more detergent to get clothes clean — hard water blunts detergent's effectiveness.

Plumbing

Gradual scale inside pipes that can reduce flow over years, and buildup on fixtures and valves. (See limescale & scale buildup.)

Appliances

Scale in the water heater (popping sounds, lost efficiency), and wear on dishwashers and washing machines. (See does hard water damage appliances?.)

Skin and hair

Tight-feeling skin and dull, coated hair after showering, because soap doesn't rinse cleanly. (See hard water, dry skin & hair — not medical advice.)

In my experience, the number alone doesn't tell the whole story. A 7 GPG result in one home may feel manageable, while 12–15 GPG often shows up as scale, spotting, and ongoing appliance maintenance. Your symptoms plus the number are what matter.

Is hard water bad for you?

For most people, no — hard water is generally not considered a direct health danger. The calcium and magnesium that make water hard are dietary minerals, not contaminants, which is why hardness isn't EPA-regulated as a health risk. What hard water affects is comfort, cleaning, plumbing, and appliances: it's a maintenance-and-comfort problem, not a safety one. (If you're managing a specific skin condition, that's a separate question for a dermatologist — hard water may affect how skin feels, which I cover carefully here.) So the honest framing is: you don't need to fear your hard water, but you may want to treat it for the maintenance and comfort wins.

When does hard water become a problem?

There's an important difference between technically hard water and water that's hard enough to justify treating. Technically, anything over 7 GPG is "hard." But whether it's a problem for your home depends on how it shows up: a home at 7–8 GPG with good habits (rinse aid, regular cleaning) might find it perfectly manageable, while the same number in another home with a tankless heater and a lot of glassware feels like a constant battle. The tipping point is usually when you're spending real time and money fighting scale, spots, and soap issues — that's when "technically hard" becomes "worth fixing." Cost climbs with hardness: above 10.5 GPG, scale-related maintenance and efficiency losses start to add up meaningfully.

Common mistakes when reading water hardness results

The errors I see most often — easy to avoid once you know them:

  • Confusing ppm with GPG. A "150" reading is alarming as GPG but normal as ppm (≈8.8 GPG). Always check the unit.
  • Treating hardness like a safety contaminant. It's a maintenance issue, not a health hazard — don't panic-buy.
  • Buying a softener without testing. You might oversize, undersize, or not need one. Test first.
  • Ignoring iron, manganese, sulfur, or chlorine. These need different treatment than hardness; a softener alone won't fix them.
  • Assuming a filter and a softener do the same job. Filters handle taste/odor/particles; softeners remove hardness. Different tools.
  • Using city-average data instead of testing your home. Your tap can differ from the city average — confirm at the source.

Final verdict: what is considered hard water?

Here's the clean summary to take away. Water is considered hard at 7 GPG or 120 ppm and above, and very hard at 10.5 GPG or 180 ppm and above. Below 1 GPG (17 ppm) is soft. Remember the one conversion that unlocks every report — 1 GPG = 17.1 ppm — and you can read your city report, your lab result, or a softener spec sheet with equal confidence. Hardness is a comfort-and-maintenance question, not a safety one: above 7 GPG you'll usually see real effects, and above 10.5 GPG treatment starts to clearly pay back in protection and convenience.

The bottom line

Hard water doesn't have to be a mystery. Match your number to the scale, translate the units with the 17.1 rule, and read it against the symptoms you're actually living with. The honest next step isn't to buy anything — it's to know your number. Once you know your hardness in GPG or ppm, choosing the right treatment (or deciding you don't need any) becomes much easier. Test first, understand the number, then decide whether treatment is worth it for your home.
